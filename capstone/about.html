About Us

This website was founded by Prof. Steve Trewick of Massey University in 2019. It is one of many that provides scientific information into the global issue of the Anthropocene, i.e. the over-use of our planet’s limited resources.

Our team began the development of the New Zealand Centre for Planetary Ecology which seeks to contribute to an effective, consistent, and orderly sustainability transition for Aotearoa/New Zealand’s people, economy, and environment. Importantly this national level initiative is couched in terms of the global perspective and draws on a concept called the nine planetary boundaries. Some of our activity in this space can be viewed at: https://blog.planetaryecology.org.

The problem is very big, too big to manage and indeed too big for us to fully comprehend. So, we set up the Turitea Project to look at ways to visualise, communicate, share, and inform on a small section of local geography. In part, this is aimed at the local community, in part a wider audience, and in part identifying what is needed and how difficult it is to communicate the issues.

The Turitea stream begins its journey in Tararua Ranges of the Manawatu region of New Zealand, and finally flows into the Manawatu river. It is a living and dynamic system although this is rarely recognised by most people. The stream and its catchment has all the components that could be seen on any part of the planet or indeed the planet as a whole. The te reo Māori translation means clear bright water. So we can view the Turitea as a biological system, a geographic/hydrological system, a water resource, or as simple a metaphor.

Within the Turitea catchment, water accumulates from rain, flowing over and through plants, animals, microbes, fungi and soil before it reaches the path of the stream, which itself moves over time by eroding and depositing sediment. At any one moment the water in the stream is not the same water as at other times; the life in it (from bacteria to multicellular creatures), the sediment, and the dissolved chemicals are constantly changing even if the average characteristics of the stream are often described as if they are fixed. For example, we know that the amount of dissolved oxygen is a good indicator of how much life there is in the water, but very different measures of oxygen are gained at night, day, morning, evening, after heavy rain, and after drought.

It is the often rare and extreme levels that dictates much of the biological composition and biodiversity. The way the physical and biological aspects of the catchment interact can appear static but we know that small and large scale change is constantly going on. Some is a variation around a mean, but the extent and skew of that variation is important. Some is unusual variation. For example, the shape of the catchment with a series of nearly level terraces at different elevations is the product of the ancestral stream winding across the landscape and carving its way over many thousands of years. The fact that most of the catchment is now clothed in grassland is the result of fires set by people in the last 200 years to remove the forest that once flourished here. How it looks and functions now is not how it did in the past or will be in the future.

In order for people to understand their role in the environment they need to see how their activity or lack of it impacts that environment. Simply then, the task for this project is find ways to bring this discussion to life.
